[Conclusions](#conclusions) --- name: overview # Overview -- ## Concept **User Experience Design** (UxD), also known as Customer Experience Design (CxD), Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), or Human-Centered Design, is the practice of *incorporating people's needs into the design* of the software, documents, events, activities, or other experiences. --- template: overview ## Assumptions We design for people with a few assumptions in mind: -- - **People use things for a reason**: typically a desire or need that they are trying to fulfill. -- - **Creators have motives for making things**: often profit, esteem from peers, "keeping up-with-the-Jones's", etc. -- - **Technologies all have constraints**: every tool has its inherent limitations that restrict what can be done with it. --- template: overview ## Everyone loves Venn diagrams... ... and User Experience Designers try to make all parties happy. This inevitably involves compromise. ![Venn diagram](../images/ux_venn_diagram.png) --- name: requirements # Requirements Gathering -- ## Concept The first thing a UX Designer must do is establish what must be done - this is **requirements gathering**. -- They use all resources available to them in order to determine the business goals, user needs, and technology constraints, including: -- - **Stakeholder interviews** - speak with the important people at the organization for whom the product is being designed. -- - **Competitive analysis** - see what the competitors are doing in this same space. -- - **User interviews and observation** - speak with potential users and observe them while they go about doing the things your tool is intended to facilitate. -- - **Demographic research** - review market research to understand the typical desires, problems, and needs of the average person in the demographic(s) you are targeting. --- name: site-maps # Site Maps -- ## Concept Site maps are diagrams intended to show the number of categories of pages that exist in a given web site and how many pages exist or must be built within each category. --- template: site-maps ## Example An example of a simple site map of a shoe shop web site. ![Site map example](../images/ux_site_map_example.png) --- template: site-maps ## Communication goals Site maps diagrams are simple, fast, easy-to-draw diagrams that communicate... -- - what **categories** of pages exist in a site. -- - the number of unique design **templates** that must be created within each category. -- - the **number of levels** of pages within each category, if a user were to *drill down* step-by-step from each category *landing page* to its children pages. --- name: wireframes # Wireframe diagrams -- ## Concept There are many types of specialized diagrams produced by User Experience Designers to serve different communication needs: -- - flow diagrams - ecosystem maps - user journeys - and more... -- For our purposes - designing and developing web pages quickly and mostly on our own - **wireframe diagrams** are the easiest to understand and produce, and provide the greatest benefit. --- template: wireframes ## Example An example of a perfect wireframe diagram representing a web page. ![Wireframe diagram example](../images/ux_wireframe_example.jpg) --- template: wireframes ## Communication goals Wireframe diagrams are simple, fast, easy-to-draw diagrams that communicate... -- - what **type of content** will be on each screen. -- - the approximate **position and size** of each piece of content. -- - **notes** about any content or interactive behavior that is not obvious just looking at the diagram --- template: wireframes ## Prohibitions Wireframes, in order to best serve their purpose, should not include... -- - **color** - everything should be black, white, and gray -- - **design** - no shadows, fancy fonts, logos, icons or other graphics -- - **real images** - use placeholder boxes instead where an image would go -- - **real text** (also known as 'copy') - use '[lorem ipsum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorem_ipsum)' placeholder copy instead --- template: wireframes ## Number Each wireframe diagram acts as a template that is then re-used for one or more screens. Most web designers will produce distinct wireframe diagrams for at least each of the following... -- - **home page** - these are usually unique and follow a different template from other pages. -- - **each level of the site map** - web pages at the same depth level will generally follow the same, or similar, template - one for each level. -- - **unique pages** - some pages are, by nature, somewhat unique in content and/or layout - for example "Contact Us" pages with *forms*, marketing *brochure-like* pages intended to give a unique look-and-feel, and others... -- - **overlays** - any content that appears superimposed over other content when something is clicked or moused over, such as a *hamburger menu*. --- name: conclusions # Conclusions -- At this point you have a just enough information to get into trouble designing site maps, wireframes, and prototypes. -- - Thank you.
